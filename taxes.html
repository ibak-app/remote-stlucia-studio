Taxes for Remote Workers in Saint Lucia

Understanding your tax obligations as a remote worker in Saint Lucia is not optional — and the answer depends almost entirely on how long you stay. Here is what you need to know before you book that one-way ticket.

Tax Overview for Remote Workers

The good news: most remote workers who stay in Saint Lucia for a short-to-medium-term visit face minimal local tax obligations if they remain below the 183-day threshold. The complexity arises for longer stays and for citizens of countries with worldwide taxation regimes (particularly the United States).

The 183-Day Rule — The Most Important Number

Under Saint Lucian law, an individual who spends 183 days or more in Saint Lucia in a tax year (January 1 to December 31) is considered a tax resident for that year. This is the single most important number for remote workers planning their stay.

Under 183 Days (Non-Resident)
  • You are NOT a Saint Lucia tax resident
  • Only Saint Lucian-source income is taxed in Saint Lucia
  • Foreign employer income generally not subject to local tax
  • You remain a tax resident of your home country
  • Home country tax obligations continue as normal
  • Simpler situation for most short-to-medium term nomads
  • Most remote workers aim to stay in this category
183+ Days (Tax Resident)
  • You ARE a Saint Lucia tax resident for that year
  • Saint Lucia can tax your worldwide income in principle
  • Must file a Saint Lucia tax return (January 31 deadline)
  • Progressive income tax applies: 10% to 30%
  • Home country may also have claims on your income
  • Potential double taxation risk if no treaty applies
  • Professional advice becomes essential at this point
📅
Counting your days Days are typically counted as days of physical presence in Saint Lucia. Both arrival and departure days may count depending on the specific interpretation. If you are planning to stay close to the 183-day threshold, keep a precise travel diary with passport stamps and boarding passes. This documentation can be critical in a tax residency dispute.

Saint Lucia Income Tax Rates

If you become a Saint Lucia tax resident, the following progressive income tax rates apply for the 2025/2026 tax year. Note that Saint Lucia's fiscal year runs April 1 to March 31, but personal income tax is assessed on the calendar year (January 1 to December 31).

Effective tax rates are lower than marginal rates Because Saint Lucia uses a progressive tax system, the effective rate (tax paid as a percentage of total income) is significantly lower than the headline rate for any given bracket. A remote worker earning EC$200,000 (~US$74,000) annually does not pay 30% on all of it — only on the portion above EC$100,000. The effective rate on EC$200,000 would be approximately 19–22%. Tax rates and allowances should be verified with a local tax professional as they may change.

Remote Workers: The Gray Area

Here is where it gets genuinely complicated. A remote worker who becomes a Saint Lucia tax resident but is employed by or contracting for a company in another country sits in a legal gray area that Saint Lucia's tax law has not fully addressed.

Under the Income Tax Act of Saint Lucia, a tax resident is liable to pay income tax on their worldwide income. This means that if you are a Saint Lucia tax resident and you earn income from a US, UK, or European employer, that income is technically subject to Saint Lucia income tax.

In practice, the enforcement of this principle against foreign-employed remote workers has been inconsistent. Most remote workers who become tax resident simply do not register with the Inland Revenue Division (IRD) and do not file returns, relying on the practical reality that Saint Lucia has limited enforcement capacity and interest in pursuing foreign-source income earned by temporary residents.

This does not mean ignoring the law is advisable. It means the current situation is ambiguous and you should seek proper advice.

The vast majority of digital nomads and remote workers in Saint Lucia who stay under 183 days face no meaningful Saint Lucian tax exposure. Their home country remains the primary tax jurisdiction. This is the situation most remote workers are in and it is relatively clean.

For those who stay longer and cross into tax residency, the pragmatic approach adopted by many is to continue filing and paying taxes in their home country, maintain documentation of their status, and consult a local accountant if they wish to formalise their situation. The key risk for longer-term stays is creating an inadvertent double taxation situation.

This is the clearest situation. If you are employed by a Saint Lucian company and physically working in Saint Lucia, your income is Saint Lucian-source income regardless of your residency status. Your employer is required to deduct income tax under the PAYE (Pay As You Earn) system and remit to the Inland Revenue Division. NIS (National Insurance Scheme) contributions also apply to locally employed individuals.

US Citizens: Special Obligations

US citizens (and Green Card holders) face a unique situation anywhere in the world: the United States taxes its citizens on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Living in Saint Lucia does not exempt you from US tax obligations. This is one of only two countries globally that taxes based on citizenship rather than residency (the other being Eritrea).

US citizens living abroad must file a US federal tax return annually (Form 1040) regardless of where they live or where their income is earned. There is no minimum income threshold for the filing requirement for citizens abroad. The deadline for US expats is June 15 (with automatic extension to October 15 available).

This obligation applies whether you stay 1 month or 10 years in Saint Lucia. The fact that you are living and working remotely in the Caribbean does not suspend your US filing obligation.

US citizens living abroad may be able to exclude a significant portion of their foreign-earned income from US taxes using the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE, Form 2555). For 2025, the FEIE exclusion is approximately US$126,500. To qualify, you must meet either the Bona Fide Residence Test (established residence in a foreign country) or the Physical Presence Test (330 days in a 12-month period outside the US).

Important: the FEIE applies to earned income (salary, self-employment). It does not apply to passive income (dividends, rental income, retirement distributions). And it does not eliminate self-employment tax obligations.

US citizens who have foreign bank accounts with an aggregate value exceeding US$10,000 at any point during the calendar year must file an FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) annually. If you open a Saint Lucian bank account (Bank of Saint Lucia, CIBC FCIB, Republic Bank), and the combined balance of all your foreign accounts exceeds US$10,000, FBAR reporting is mandatory.

FBAR penalties for non-compliance are severe — up to US$10,000 per non-wilful violation and substantially more for wilful violations. This is a compliance requirement that should be taken seriously.

FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) requires US citizens with foreign financial assets above certain thresholds to report them on Form 8938. Thresholds vary by filing status and residency. Saint Lucian financial institutions may also report US account holders to the IRS under FATCA inter-governmental agreements. US citizens should not expect financial privacy from US tax authorities when banking in Saint Lucia.

🇺🇸
US citizens: use a specialist expat tax advisor US expat tax is genuinely complex and the penalties for non-compliance are significant. Use a qualified CPA or tax attorney who specialises in expat taxation. General tax preparers often lack the specific knowledge required. Look for preparers familiar with Form 2555, FBAR, Form 8938, and the specific interplay between US and Caribbean tax obligations.

Other Nationalities

Non-US citizens generally have a simpler situation, but your home country's rules still apply. Here is a general orientation for the most common nationalities of remote workers in Saint Lucia.

UK residents are taxed by HMRC based on tax residency, not citizenship. If you leave the UK and establish tax residency elsewhere, you may be able to reduce or eliminate UK income tax obligations. The UK Statutory Residence Test (SRT) determines your UK residency status. Becoming a non-UK resident typically requires spending fewer than 183 days in the UK in a tax year (April 6 to April 5) and meeting other conditions.

If you spend 183+ days in Saint Lucia and can establish Saint Lucia tax residency, it may be possible to become a UK non-resident. The UK has no double taxation agreement with Saint Lucia, so careful planning is needed to avoid double taxation on any UK-source income.

Canada taxes based on residency, not citizenship. Canadians who establish genuine non-residency status (including tax residency in another country) can significantly reduce Canadian tax obligations. The CRA (Canada Revenue Agency) uses a "significant residential ties" test — owning a home, having a spouse in Canada, and other factors can maintain Canadian residency even if you are physically abroad.

Canada and Saint Lucia do not have a double taxation agreement. Canadian remote workers spending extended periods in Saint Lucia should get professional advice on severing or maintaining Canadian residency before departure.

EU countries vary significantly in their approach to citizens working abroad. Some countries (like Germany) have strict rules about maintaining residency obligations even when living abroad. Others are more flexible. Generally, EU citizens who establish genuine tax residency in Saint Lucia can transition their tax obligations, but the process and requirements vary by country. Consult a tax advisor in your specific EU member state.

Citizens of CARICOM member states (Barbados, Trinidad, Jamaica, Grenada, and others) moving to Saint Lucia for work generally have more straightforward residency pathways under CARICOM free movement rights. CARICOM nationals who are skilled workers can access Saint Lucia on a CARICOM skills certificate. Tax obligations would generally follow from establishing Saint Lucian residency and local employment. CARICOM workers employed by Saint Lucian employers would be subject to normal PAYE and NIS provisions.

NIS — National Insurance Scheme

Saint Lucia's National Insurance Scheme (NIS) is a social security system providing retirement, sickness, maternity, and employment injury benefits. Understanding when it applies to remote workers is important.

NIS practical note Remote workers employed by foreign companies and not registered as Saint Lucian employees are generally not subject to NIS contributions. NIS primarily applies to the employer-employee relationship within Saint Lucia. If you are not registered as an employee of a Saint Lucian entity, NIS contributions are typically not required — though this may change if you formalise your tax residency situation.

Double Taxation Agreements

Double taxation agreements (DTAs, also called tax treaties) allow countries to coordinate their tax systems so that taxpayers are not taxed twice on the same income. Saint Lucia's DTA network is very limited compared to larger economies.

CountryDTA with Saint Lucia?Practical Implication
United States No treaty US tax credits mechanism available but no formal treaty protection
United Kingdom No treaty Risk of double taxation on income earned by UK-source payers
Canada No treaty Careful residency planning required to avoid double taxation
CARICOM member states Regional agreements CARICOM has regional tax coordination provisions for member states
European Union members Very limited Most EU countries have no specific DTA with Saint Lucia
💡
Foreign tax credits as a mitigating mechanism Even without a formal double taxation agreement, many countries (including the US, UK, and Canada) allow their residents to claim a foreign tax credit for taxes paid to another country. This means that if you pay income tax in Saint Lucia, you may be able to reduce your home country tax bill by the amount paid in Saint Lucia, avoiding true double taxation. The interaction is complex and varies by country — professional advice is essential.

Practical Tax Planning for Remote Workers

Here is actionable guidance based on the most common situations. None of this is legal advice — use it as a framework for your conversation with a qualified professional.

The simplest tax situation for most remote workers is to stay under 183 days in Saint Lucia in any calendar year. This keeps your home country tax residency intact and means you have no meaningful Saint Lucian tax obligations on your foreign-source income. You continue filing and paying taxes in your home country as normal.

Practical tips: Count your days carefully. Keep your passport and boarding pass records. Enter Saint Lucia multiple times across two calendar years to extend your total Caribbean time while staying under the threshold in each year. For example, arriving November 1 and leaving May 31 spans 213 days but only 61 days in each calendar year.

If you intend to stay in Saint Lucia for 6 months or more, plan your tax situation proactively before you arrive. Key steps:

1. Consult a tax advisor in your home country about the implications of becoming a non-resident for tax purposes. 2. Understand what ties need to be severed (home ownership, bank accounts, pension contributions) and what can remain. 3. Consult a Saint Lucian accountant or tax professional about local registration and filing requirements. 4. Maintain comprehensive records of days present, income earned, and tax paid in both jurisdictions.

Saint Lucia has a small but professional accounting sector. For tax advice specific to Saint Lucia, look for members of the Institute of Chartered Accountants of the Eastern Caribbean (ICAEC). Many firms in Castries and Rodney Bay offer tax consultation services. Expect to pay EC$500–1,500 (~US$185–556) for an initial tax consultation.

For US expat tax specifically, international firms with Caribbean expertise or US-based expat tax specialists (many operate remotely) are often a better starting point than local Saint Lucian accountants who may have limited familiarity with US tax law.

Regardless of your situation, maintain detailed records: passport stamps and entry/exit dates (photograph every stamp), boarding passes and flight records, proof of home country tax filings, employment contracts or client agreements showing the foreign source of your income, bank statements showing where income was received, and any Saint Lucian receipts for taxes paid. These records protect you in the unlikely event of a tax residency dispute in either jurisdiction.
