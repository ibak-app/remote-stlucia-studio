Connectivity Overview

Saint Lucia's internet infrastructure has improved significantly over the past five years. Urban areas — particularly Castries, Rodney Bay, Gros Islet, and Vieux Fort — now have access to genuine fiber broadband. Rural and mountainous areas lag considerably, often relying on DSL or mobile data. The key variable for remote workers is location within the island.

50–100 Mbps fiber (urban)
10–25 Mbps DSL (rural)
2 Major ISPs
4G LTE widespread
💡
The most important thing to know Always test internet at your specific rental address before committing to a long lease. The same neighbourhood can have radically different speeds depending on whether fibre has been pulled to that specific building. Ask the landlord for a speed test screenshot — or better, visit in person with your laptop.

ISPs — Flow vs Digicel

Saint Lucia has two primary internet service providers competing for residential and business customers. Both offer fiber in urban areas and maintain 4G mobile networks across most of the island.

Flow (Liberty Latin America)
  • Type: Fiber + DSL + cable
  • Coverage: Broadest fiber footprint
  • Urban speeds: 50–200 Mbps fiber
  • Reliability: Generally consistent
  • Business plans: Available
  • Customer service: Improved in recent years
  • Best for: Home fiber broadband
  • Website: discoverflow.co/slu
Digicel
  • Type: Fiber + mobile broadband
  • Coverage: Strong mobile network
  • Urban speeds: 50–100 Mbps fiber
  • Reliability: Good, strong on mobile
  • Business plans: Available
  • Customer service: Mixed reviews
  • Best for: Mobile data backup SIM
  • Website: digicelgroup.com/lc
💡
Dual-provider strategy recommended Many long-term remote workers in Saint Lucia use Flow for home fibre and Digicel (or vice versa) for their mobile data SIM. Having both providers means you have a fallback when one experiences an outage or maintenance window.

Real-World Speeds

Advertised speeds and real-world speeds can differ, especially at peak hours. Here is what remote workers actually experience.

On a 100 Mbps fiber plan in Rodney Bay, real-world speeds typically land between 40–80 Mbps during business hours, with peak-evening speeds occasionally dipping to 20–40 Mbps as more households come online. Morning and off-peak hours often yield speeds close to the advertised maximum.

For video conferencing (Zoom, Teams, Meet), you need a consistent 5–10 Mbps upload. On fibre in urban areas, this is reliably achievable. Latency to US East Coast servers is typically 40–80ms — acceptable for most remote work including VoIP calls.

Video calls: Reliable on fibre in urban areas
File uploads: Good — typically 10–40 Mbps upload on fibre
VPN performance: Some reduction but manageable

DSL in Saint Lucia ranges from frustrating to workable depending on line quality and proximity to the exchange. Expect 5–20 Mbps down and 1–5 Mbps up in the best cases. Video calls are possible but buffer; large file transfers take significant time.

If your accommodation has DSL, strongly invest in a reliable 4G mobile hotspot as your primary connection and use DSL as a secondary. Many remote workers in semi-rural areas find that a Digicel or Flow LTE connection on a good signal outperforms their fixed DSL line.

To test your internet connection speed, visit speedtest.net or fast.com. Run tests at multiple times of day — morning (8–10 AM), midday (12–2 PM), and evening (7–9 PM). This gives you a realistic picture of your connection's consistency throughout the day.

For latency to your work tools, specifically test connectivity to your key services: Google Workspace, AWS, Microsoft Azure, Slack, Zoom. Caribbean-routed traffic typically hops through Miami or New York data centres.

Mobile Data Plans

Both Flow and Digicel offer prepaid and postpaid SIM cards with data plans. Mobile 4G LTE coverage is good across populated areas. This is your essential backup when fixed internet fails.

Flow Postpaid unlimited Unlimited (throttled after 30GB) EC$180–250 US$67–93
Digicel Postpaid unlimited Unlimited (throttled after 25GB) EC$160–230 US$59–85

Both Flow and Digicel have retail stores in Castries (the capital), Rodney Bay, and Vieux Fort. You can purchase a SIM on the same day you arrive. Bring your passport for identification. Prepaid SIMs typically cost EC$10–25 including some initial credit.

SIM registration is required by law — you will need to provide your passport details. The process takes about 15 minutes at a retail location. Once registered, you can top up online, via mobile app, or at any of hundreds of small shops island-wide that sell mobile credit.

Both providers offer mobile hotspot tethering on their SIMs, which is useful for laptop connectivity. Ensure your plan explicitly allows tethering — some promotional plans restrict it.

4G LTE coverage is strong along all major roads and in every town. The main gaps are in the mountainous interior — the rainforest areas including the Quilesse Forest Reserve, remote valleys, and some elevated rural communities. For the vast majority of remote workers who base themselves in urban or coastal areas, 4G is reliable throughout the day.

Signal is typically excellent in Castries, Rodney Bay, Gros Islet, Vieux Fort, Soufriere, and Micoud. Minor degradation can occur during heavy rain (temporary attenuation) but normally resolves quickly.

Check the live coverage maps at: discoverflow.co/slu and digicelgroup.com/lc/coverage

Starlink in Saint Lucia

SpaceX's Starlink satellite internet service is available in the Caribbean and represents a game-changer for remote workers in rural or underserved areas of Saint Lucia.

Yes. Starlink has expanded availability across the Caribbean region, and Saint Lucia is within serviceable coverage. As of 2026, residential Starlink service is available, though regulatory considerations in the region may affect availability. Check starlink.com for current availability at your specific address.

Starlink works best with a clear sky view — not always easy in Saint Lucia's mountainous terrain. Locations with unobstructed views of the sky perform best. Dense tree cover or steep hillsides can cause interference.

Starlink residential service typically delivers 50–200 Mbps download speeds with 10–30 Mbps upload and latency of 20–60ms. This is comparable to or better than DSL, and competitive with lower-tier fibre in some locations.

The hardware kit (dish, router, cables) costs approximately US$499 to purchase. Monthly service is approximately US$120/month for residential. This is significantly more expensive than local ISPs, but may be the only reliable option for rural or mountainous properties where no fibre exists.

For remote workers in Soufriere, the interior, or rural coastal areas, Starlink can be the difference between being able to work effectively and not. The investment pays off quickly if it enables you to live in a location you love.

For urban and suburban locations with good fibre access, local ISPs (Flow or Digicel) are significantly cheaper and typically have lower latency. Use Starlink if you need reliable internet in an area without decent fibre, or as a premium backup to your primary fixed connection.

Some remote workers in Saint Lucia run a hybrid: Flow or Digicel fibre as primary, Starlink as automatic failover. This combination provides near-100% uptime even during utility company outages — the ideal setup for anyone doing live client work.

Power & Outage Planning

LUCELEC (Lucia Electricity Services Limited) is the island's sole electricity provider. The grid is reasonably reliable but outages do occur, particularly during storms, high demand periods, or infrastructure maintenance. Planning for this is essential for remote workers.

Outages in urban areas are typically brief — 30 minutes to 2 hours — and happen perhaps once or twice a month under normal conditions. During tropical storms or hurricane season (June–November), they can be more frequent and longer-lasting. Rural areas experience more frequent and longer outages than urban centres.

LUCELEC does announce planned maintenance outages in advance, typically via local radio stations and their social media. Joining the LUCELEC outage notification list helps you plan critical work sessions around scheduled downtime.

For a remote worker, a UPS is essential, not optional. At minimum, get a UPS that can power your router and laptop for 1–2 hours. This covers the majority of brief outages and allows you to finish calls and save work gracefully.

A good UPS can be purchased locally from hardware and electronics stores in Castries or Rodney Bay. Budget EC$300–800 (~US$111–296) for a unit suitable for a home office setup (router + laptop + monitor). Quality brands (APC, Eaton, CyberPower) are available. This is one of the best investments you will make as a remote worker in Saint Lucia.

Recommended setup: UPS (1000–1500VA) connected to router, modem, and laptop charger. This provides 1–3 hours of run time depending on load.

Caribbean power supplies can exhibit voltage fluctuations — brief spikes or dips — particularly before an outage or when power is restored. Standard power strips without surge protection offer no protection against these events. Always use a quality surge protector or UPS with automatic voltage regulation (AVR).

Saint Lucia uses 120V/60Hz electricity with Type A and B outlets (identical to USA/Canada). North American devices work without adapters. European or UK devices need adapters and potentially voltage converters (though most modern laptop power bricks are universal 100–240V).

For long-term residents and those in more rural properties, rooftop solar is increasingly popular in Saint Lucia due to strong year-round sunlight. Several local companies offer solar installation. A basic home solar system can provide grid independence for an office setup and is a worthwhile long-term investment if you plan to stay 6+ months.

Portable petrol generators (EC$800–3,000, ~US$300–1,100) are an option but impractical for apartment dwellers due to noise, fumes, and storage requirements. They are more common in villa or standalone house settings. Inverter generators are quieter but more expensive.

⚡
Hurricane season: June to November Saint Lucia is in the hurricane belt, though it sits toward the southern end where direct hurricane hits are historically less common than islands like Dominica or Barbuda. Nonetheless, tropical storms can cause multi-day outages and internet disruption. Have a plan — extra water, charged devices, and mobile data loaded up — before June each year.
